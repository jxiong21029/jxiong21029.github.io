title: "Thoughts On: Advantage Weighted Actor-Critic"
---

Advantage weighted actor-critic ([AWAC](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.09359)) is an algorithm to
approach the problem of finetuning an agent online while leveraging an offline (potentially
suboptimal) demonstration dataset.

## Existing Alternatives

1. Pretraining with behavioral cloning (BC) and finetuning with on-policy RL
    - Behavioral cloning typically assumes optimal demonstrations
    - Discarding the demonstration data after initialization is data-inefficient
2. Standard off-policy RL (e.g. [SAC](https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.01290)) with demonstration data
   inserted into the replay buffer
    - Inserting the demonstration data does not improve performance due to error accumulation in
        off-policy bootstrapping
    - If the action \\( a' \sim \pi \\) used to update \\( Q(s, a) \\) lies outside the data
        distribution, \\( Q(s, a') \\) may be inaccurate
3. Standard offline RL (e.g. [BEAR](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.00949)) with additional, streaming data
    - Typically utilize an estimate of the dataset's behavior policy for constrained optimization
    - Once the online policy begins to diverge from the demonstrated policy, the updates can become
        too conservative, resulting in slow learning

## AWAC: Advantage weighted actor-critic

- Critic is trained off-policy (standard \\( \text{TD}(0) \\) to estimate advantages \\( A^\pi \\),
    which are used to optimize the actor \\( \pi\_\theta \\) s.t. at iteration \\( k \\):

\\[ \theta_{k + 1} = \text{argmax}\_{\theta} \; \mathbb{E}\_{s, a \sim \beta} \left[ \log
\pi\_{\theta} (a \mid s) \exp \left(\frac{1}{\lambda} A^{\pi\_{\theta_k}} (s, a)\right) \right] \\]

- \\( \beta \\) is a replay buffer which includes both the offline demo data and online
    trajectories, and the Lagrange multiplier \\( \lambda \\) is treated as a hyperparameter.

- Essentially a supervised learning update where targets are re-weighted by the critic at every
    iteration, avoiding explicit parametric modeling of the behavior policy

## My Thoughts

One baseline for comparison I would have liked to see in this paper is an agent trained with a
standard RL algorithm with an additional KL objective with respect to a behaviorally cloned policy,

\\[ \mathcal{L}\_{klpt} = \rho D\_{\text{KL}}(\pi\_{\text{BC}} \mid\mid \pi\_\theta) \\]
where \\( \rho \\) is a coefficient to weight the loss relative to other losses.

This approach is relatively simple to implement and empirically effective: it set SOTA Minecraft
results in the recent [VPT paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.11795) and a similar student-teacher
version was used during the [Lux AI challenge](https://www.kaggle.com/competitions/lux-ai-2021) to
train the [first place solution](https://www.kaggle.com/competitions/lux-ai-2021/discussion/294993).

As a further baseline, it is plausible to replace \\( \pi_\text{BC} \\) with a policy obtained by
offline RL. Could tuning or utilizing a scheduler for \\( \rho \\) overcome the aforementioned
issues with slow online learning after offline pretraining?

With regards to the problem space, I am also interested in methods to pretrain in a
*self-supervised* fashion, perhaps learning useful representations or building high-level intuition
which could accelerate online learning or be useful in a zero-shot setting. This approach could lend
itself more easily to diverse sources of data: perhaps leveraging demonstrations involving slightly
different environments or tasks, or perhaps those provided by experts with heterogeneous observation
or action spaces.
